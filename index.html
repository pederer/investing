Compound Interest

If Alex borrows $10,000 to purchase a car, Alex is expected to pay back the loan "with interest". Interest is the cost of borrowing money. Alex agreed to a 5% Annual Percentage Rate (APR) of interest on the loan, but does not make any payments; after one year Alex owes $10,500, and after two years he owes $11025.

1 Year: $10,000 x 5% = $500 in interest
2 Years: $10,500 x 5% = $525 in compound interest

Alex owes more in interest the second year because he is paying interest on both the initial principal and the previously accumulated interest; this is called Compound Interest. In this context, Compound Interest is very bad because it causes your debt to grow at an exponential rate. However, you may have heard of the phrase "Compound Interest" used in a positive way; this is because many people confuse Compound Interest with a similar concept: Compound Rate of Return.

Compound Rate of Return

Although the example above shows why unpaid debt can accumulate quickly, we can employ the same principles to investing to see how an investment can grow over time. If we earn income from an investment but reinvest our income back into the investment, the next income we earn from the investment will be based on both the principal and the previous income: this is called Compound Rate of Return. A rate of return is the gain (profit) of an investment, expressed as a percentage of the investment. Although this rate of return is not "interest", it functions in much the same way. It is common for the term "compound interest" to be applied to investment gains, though technically "compound rates of return" may be more appropriate.

Rule of 72

Compound interest / compound rates of return are hard to calculate in your head. Thankfully there is an easy-to-remember formula for determining the effects of compounding over time: The Rule of 72! Divide 72 by the rate of return (or interest rate) to get an approximation of how long it will take your principal to double.

Formula:
72 / Rate = No. of years
Example: An investment with an annual interest rate of 4% should double in approximately 18 years.
72 / 4 = 18

The formula is not 100% accurate, but it is close! Use the worksheet above to double-check.

What are stocks?

A "stock" is a security (i.e. tradable asset) that represents ownership in a company. Having a company's stock entitles the owner of the stock to voting rights within the company, and rights to the company's assets and profits, proportional to the number of stocks owned. Some companies return a share of profits to stock owners (also called shareholders) by a per-stock payout; this is called a "dividend". A company's stock changes in value over time; if a stock is sold for a price exceeding the original purchase price, the seller is said to have made gains on his original investment of capital, which is called "capital gains". Dividends and capital gains are two ways that investors attempt to profit from stocks.

What are bonds?

A "bond" can be thought of as a certificate of debt, or the record of a loan. Companies and governments issue bonds to raise money. Investors give an amount of money (called the "bond principal"), and in exchange they are given a bond which spells out what rate of interest (called the "coupon rate") the bond holder is entitled to, and when the bond principal must be repaid. A bond can usually be traded like other securities, though the value of a bond decreases over time as interest payments are made. Bonds are considered safer investments than stocks, for two reasons: first, bonds tend to have a more stable value (so long as the issuer remains solvent); second, in the case of bankrupcy, debts (including bonds) are paid before stockholders. Bonds investment gains usually lag behind stocks over the long term. However, bonds are often used in low-risk investment portfolios; a retired investor may be able to live off of the stable income of bond intrest without worrying as much about the state of the stock exchange.

What are mutual funds?

A "mutual fund" is a managed bundle (or portfolio) of stocks, bonds, and other securities. In most cases, a mutual fund includes a large number of securities, with each individual security weighted proportionally according to the fund manager's decision. A mutual fund's value is tied to securities within the fund, so if Company Alpha's stock value falls then the value of a mutual fund's share would fall proportionally. However, because a mutual fund's portfolio includes many different securities, the impact of one stock's negative or positive performance has a muted impact on the mutual fund as a whole. This is why mutual funds are often described as a "diversified investment". The method in which an investor profits from a mutual fund depends on the types of securities within the portfolio: some securities appreciate (increase) in value (capital gains), pay dividends, or pay interest.

Many people invest in mutual funds because they are diversified and managed according to an investment strategy. However, there are a few drawbacks of which an investor should be aware.

  • There are fees associated with investing in a mutual fund. Different funds have vastly different types of fees, so it is important to know what those fees are, and how it will affect your investment return.
  • Mutual fund shares do not entitle the owner to voting rights for any company within the fund. This fact may not be important for every investor, but some appreciate the "ownership" commanded by actual stocks.
  • Mutual fund shares are purchased or sold once per day. If you are a long-term investor, this should not be a problem. However, short-term investors (either intentionally or accidentally) should understand that mutual fund shares are not as "liquid" (easily converted to cash) as stocks; although, they are more liquid than some other securities.

What are index funds?

A "market index" is a theoretical portfolio which represents a segment of a market. For example, the Standard and Poor's 500 Index (also called the "S&P 500") is an index of 505 of the largest companies on the stock exchange; the "market" is large capital companies. An "index fund" is a mutual fund which is managed according to the rules of an index; the stocks, and proportions of stocks, identified by an index is followed by the corresponding index fund. Because the makeup of indexes are known, and changes to indexes are widely available, the cost of managing an index fund is generally lower than that of other types of mutual funds, which generally means that the fees associated with investing in an index fund are very low compared to other managed accounts.
